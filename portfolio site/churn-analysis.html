Business Problem

Uncertainty over what drives customer churn makes it challenging for telecommunications businesses to implement effective retention strategies. While attrition threatens revenue and long-term stability, the influence of service usage, contract terms, billing methods, pricing tiers, and customer demographics remains unclear.

This analysis was conducted to identify the key drivers of customer churn using a Telecom Customer Churn dataset sourced from Kaggle, with the objective of identifying the key drivers of churn and high-risk customer segments, and recommending strategies to reduce customer loss and revenue leakage.

Key Metrics

  • Churn Rate: 26.5%
  • Total Revenue: $21.37M
  • Revenue Lost to Churn: $3.68M

Key Findings

  • Churn was highest among new customers and those on month-to-month contracts, with rates reaching 59.9% and 51.7% respectively, indicating elevated churn risk during early customer lifecycle stages and among customers without long-term commitment.
  • Customers on high monthly charge tiers and those using fiber optic services recorded higher churn rates, indicating that the value delivered by premium offerings may not consistently align with customer expectations.
  • Churn was higher among customers using paperless billing and manual payment methods, with mailed check users recording the highest churn, indicating that billing experience alone does not drive retention without efficient payment processes.
  • Customers without value-added services recorded significantly higher churn, while churn declined as the number of subscribed services increased, indicating that deeper service adoption strengthens customer retention.
  • Customer churn was primarily driven by competitive factors, with 45% of churn attributed to competitors offering better devices and promotional incentives, highlighting the impact of external market competition on customer retention.

Recommendations

  • Improve Early Customer Retention: Strengthen onboarding support and incentivize migration from month-to-month contracts to longer-term plans.
  • Enhance Value Alignment for Premium Services: Improve retention among premium customers by strengthening the alignment between pricing and the value delivered by high-tier plans and premium services.
  • Optimize Billing and Payment Experience: Encourage adoption of automated payment methods to reduce friction associated with manual payment processes.
  • Increase Service Adoption and Bundling: Promote value-added services and bundled offerings to deepen customer engagement and reduce churn risk.
  • Address Competition-Driven Churn: Enhance product value and service quality to compete effectively with alternatives driving customer churn.

1. Introduction

Customer churn remains a critical challenge for telecommunications companies, with direct implications for revenue stability and growth. When customers leave, the impact extends beyond lost revenue to increased acquisition costs and reduced customer lifetime value. This analysis examines customer churn data to identify the key drivers of attrition and translate analytical findings into recommendations that support retention strategy and revenue protection.

2. Problem Statement

Uncertainty over what drives customer churn makes it challenging for telecommunications businesses to implement effective retention strategies. While attrition threatens revenue and long-term stability, the influence of service usage, contract terms, billing methods, pricing tiers, and customer demographics remains unclear. This analysis seeks to identify the key drivers of churn and high-risk customer segments, and provide recommendations to support retention strategies and improve customer loyalty.

3. Problem Objectives

The objective of this analysis is to examine the drivers of customer churn and factors associated with higher churn risk across customer segments. Specifically, the analysis will:

  • Identify high-risk customer segments across tenure, contract type, and demographic profiles
  • Evaluate the influence of service type and pricing tiers on churn
  • Assess the impact of billing preferences and payment methods on churn
  • Analyze the effect of service adoption and bundling on churn
  • Determine the primary drivers of churn, including competitive and service-related factors

4. Tools and Data Source

The analysis was conducted using Microsoft Excel for data manipulation, exploratory analysis and visualization, with Power Query applied to clean and transform the dataset. The analysis is based on the Telecom Customer Churn dataset sourced from Kaggle, containing 7,043 customer records.

5. Dataset Structure and Scope

The dataset captures customer-level information across three core dimensions:

  • Customer Profile: Demographic and location attributes including age, gender, marital status, number of dependents, tenure, city, and zip code
  • Product and Service Information: Service subscription details such as internet and phone services, add-on products, contract type, billing preferences, and payment methods
  • Revenue and Churn Outcomes: Financial metrics including monthly and total charges, total revenue, customer status, churn category, and churn reason

Data Considerations

  • Some service-related fields apply only to subscribed customers, resulting in structurally missing values which reflect service eligibility rather than data quality issues
  • Customer churn analysis excludes newly joined customers from churn calculations where applicable
  • The dataset represents a cross-sectional view of customer activity and does not capture long-term behavioral changes over multiple years

6. Data Preparation and Feature Engineering

The dataset was prepared to ensure consistency, accuracy, and suitability for analysis.

Data Cleaning and Standardization

  • Missing values were handled conditionally based on service dependency rules
  • Blank numeric fields were set to zero for non-subscribed services
  • Blank categorical fields were labeled as "No" to indicate non-subscription
  • Data types were standardized, with numeric variables converted appropriately and categorical fields normalized
  • Duplicate records were removed to ensure record-level uniqueness

Feature Engineering (Calculated Columns)

  • Age Group: Customers segmented into Youth, Adult, Middle-Aged, and Senior categories
  • Churn Flag: Binary classification to distinguish churned and retained customers
  • Tenure Group: Categorized into New, Short-Term, Mid-Term, and Long-Term segments
  • Monthly Charge Tier: Segmented into Low, Mid, and High pricing groups
  • Service Count: Total number of subscribed add-on services
  • Service Bundle Category: Classified into No Service, Basic, Standard, and Premium tiers based on service adoption

7. Exploratory Data Analysis

Key Metrics

  • Churn Rate: 26.5%
  • Total Revenue: $21.37M
  • Revenue Lost to Churn: $3.68M

Customer Profile

  • Churn rates varied across demographic groups. Senior customers recorded the highest churn rate at 34.1%, while other age groups ranged between 22.3% and 26.4%.
  • Gender showed minimal variation, with churn rates of 28.7% for female customers and 28.1% for male customers.
  • Customers with higher numbers of dependents showed variability, with churn reaching 40% among customers with six to seven dependents, compared to 6.3% among those with two to three dependents.

Service Type and Pricing

  • Churn varied significantly across internet service types. Fiber optic customers recorded the highest churn at 42.1%, followed by cable at 27.5%, while DSL customers recorded the lowest churn at 20%.
  • Churn also increased with pricing tiers, with high-charge customers recording a churn rate of 34.7%, compared to 32% for mid-tier and 13.9% for low-tier customers.

Service Adoption and Bundling

  • Customers without value added services such as online security, premium tech support, and online backup recorded higher churn rates ranging from 31.94% to 34.10%.
  • Churn declined significantly as the number of subscribed services increased, dropping from 60.3% among customers without add-ons to approximately 15% among customers subscribed to premium bundles with six to eight services.

Tenure and Contract Type

  • Churn decreased as customer tenure increased. New customers recorded the highest churn at 59.9%, followed by short-term customers at 28.7%, mid-term at 20.4%, and long-term customers at 9.5%.
  • Month-to-month contracts recorded the highest churn rate at 51.7%, compared to 10.9% for one-year contracts and 2.6% for two-year contracts.

Billing and Payment Methods

  • Customers enrolled in paperless billing recorded a churn rate of 35.2%, compared to 17.9% for those without paperless billing.
  • Churn also varied across payment methods, with mailed check users recording the highest churn at 41.4%, followed by bank withdrawal at 35.6%, while credit card users recorded the lowest churn at 15.8%.

Segment Interaction Analysis

  • Contract Type and Tenure: Month-to-month contracts recorded consistently high churn across all tenure groups, reaching 68% among new customers and remaining elevated even among long-term customers.
  • Payment Method and Paperless Billing: Customers without paperless billing consistently recorded lower churn across all payment methods. Among mailed check users, churn increased from 32.4% without paperless billing to 51.2% with paperless billing.

Churn Categories and Reasons

  • Competition was the dominant driver of churn, accounting for 45% of churn cases. The most frequent reasons included competitors offering better devices and better offers, followed by service dissatisfaction such as poor support experience.

8. Insights

  • Customer churn is highest among new customers and those on month-to-month contracts, indicating that early-stage customer experience and lack of long-term commitment increase churn risk
  • Higher churn among customers on high pricing tiers and fiber optic services indicates that premium offerings do not consistently meet customer expectations
  • Increased churn among customers using paperless billing and manual payment methods indicates that billing convenience alone does not reduce churn without efficient payment processes
  • Lower churn among customers with multiple subscribed services indicates that deeper service adoption strengthens customer retention
  • Customer churn is primarily driven by competitive factors, indicating that customers are more likely to leave when alternative offerings provide better value

9. Recommendations

  • Strengthen onboarding support and incentivize migration from month-to-month contracts to longer-term plans.
  • Improve retention among premium customers by strengthening the alignment between pricing and the value delivered by high-tier plans and premium services.
  • Encourage adoption of automated payment methods to reduce friction associated with manual payment processes.
  • Promote value-added services and bundled offerings to deepen customer engagement and reduce churn risk.
  • Enhance product value and service quality to compete effectively with alternatives driving customer churn.
