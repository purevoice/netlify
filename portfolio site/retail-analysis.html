Retail Telecom Sales & Operations Analysis

Excel Power Query Sales Analysis Operations

Business Problem

Performance in retail telecom operations can be difficult to interpret when the underlying drivers of outcomes are not clearly understood. Changes in revenue alone do not provide sufficient visibility into what is influencing performance, making it challenging to accurately assess outcomes or identify areas requiring attention.

This analysis was conducted to identify the key drivers of retail sales performance using a multi-table retail telecom dataset covering a six-month period from January 2025 to June 2025, with the objective of supporting informed decision making across sales execution, product performance, and inventory management.

Key Metrics

  • Total Revenue: ₦30.55M
  • Total Transactions: 1,039
  • Total Footfall: 32,991
  • Conversion Rate: 3.15%

Key Findings

  • Sales performance was driven by fluctuations in purchasing activity, indicating that revenue outcomes are influenced by inconsistent sales execution, with periods of stronger performance reflecting temporary improvements in demand capture rather than sustained effectiveness.
  • Revenue performance relative to target suggests that while targets were achievable, inconsistent execution led to underperformance in later periods, indicating a gap in sustaining performance rather than insufficient customer demand.
  • Store performance differences suggest that revenue outcomes are influenced by variations in sales execution across locations, resulting in uneven conversion efficiency and value extraction from customer traffic, although overall conversion remains low.
  • Product performance was driven by a mix of high value and high-volume products, indicating that revenue contribution was influenced by both pricing and demand rather than volume alone.
  • Inventory levels were higher than product demand, as excess stock and low to moderate sell through rates indicate that products are not moving fast enough to match available supply.

Recommendations

  • Improve sales performance by strengthening sales execution through targeted staff training focused on converting customer footfall into purchases, supported by standardized selling approaches such as needs based selling, bundle first recommendations, and structured upselling techniques to improve conversion across stores and increase revenue.
  • Increase revenue contribution by prioritizing high value products that drive revenue, while maintaining sales of high-volume products that sustain transaction activity, ensuring a balanced product focus that supports both higher transaction value and consistent sales volume.
  • Improve inventory management by aligning stock levels with customer demand, focusing on faster-moving products and reducing excess stock of slow-moving items to ensure better product movement and minimize overstock.

1. Problem Statement

Performance in retail telecom operations can be difficult to interpret when the underlying drivers of outcomes are not clearly understood. Changes in revenue alone do not provide sufficient visibility into what is influencing performance, making it challenging to accurately assess outcomes or identify areas requiring attention.

This analysis seeks to identify the key drivers of retail sales performance and provide recommendations that support informed decision making across sales execution, product performance, and inventory management.

2. Problem Objectives

The objective of this analysis is to evaluate retail telecom performance by identifying trends, key drivers, and operational insights across stores and products. Specifically, the analysis will:

  • Examine monthly revenue performance and trends across the six-month period
  • Evaluate revenue performance relative to targets
  • Assess store performance and conversion efficiency
  • Identify top-performing and low-performing products
  • Analyze inventory levels, product availability, and sell-through rates

3. Tools and Data Source

The analysis was conducted using Microsoft Excel for data modeling and visualization, with Power Query applied for data cleaning and transformation, and Power Pivot used to establish relationships and support multi-table analysis.

The analysis is based on a synthetic retail telecom dataset covering a six-month period from January 2025 to June 2025, consisting of multiple related tables capturing transactions, footfall, targets, inventory, and supporting product and store attributes.

4. Dataset Structure and Scope

The dataset captures retail operations across multiple dimensions:

  • Transactions Data: Daily product sales across stores
  • Footfall Data: Daily customer traffic by store
  • Target Data: Monthly revenue targets by store
  • Inventory Data: Stock levels and product movement
  • Supporting Tables: Store and product attributes

Data Considerations

  • The dataset spans a six-month period, limiting long-term trend analysis
  • Data is synthetic and designed to simulate retail telecom operations
  • Daily and monthly datasets were aligned through date and time dimensions to ensure consistency

5. Data Preparation and Modeling

The dataset was prepared to ensure consistency, accuracy, and reliable multi-table analysis.

Data Cleaning and Standardization

  • Removed duplicate records to ensure accurate aggregation
  • Standardized date formats across all tables
  • Ensured numeric fields such as revenue and quantity were correctly formatted

Data Modeling

  • Built a structured data model connecting store, product, and time dimensions to fact tables
  • Created date and month dimensions to align daily and monthly data
  • Established relationships to support cross-table filtering and analysis

Calculated Metrics

The following metrics were created to support analysis:

  • Revenue
  • Transaction Count
  • Total Footfall
  • Conversion Rate
  • Target Revenue
  • Variance (Actual vs Target)
  • Units Sold
  • Available Stock
  • Sell-Through Rate

Monthly Revenue Trend

Revenue fluctuated moderately across the six-month period, indicating stable but non-linear performance. It started at approximately ₦5.3M in January, declined to about ₦4.4M in February, and then recovered gradually, reaching a peak of approximately ₦5.7M in May before slightly declining to ₦5.5M in June.

Revenue vs Target Performance

Revenue performance relative to target showed a clear shift across the period.

  • January to March recorded positive variances of approximately ₦325.9K, ₦166.2K, and ₦180.5K respectively
  • April to June recorded negative variances of approximately ₦176.3K, ₦92.7K, and ₦95.8K respectively

Store Performance and Conversion Rate

Store performance varied across locations in terms of both revenue contribution and conversion efficiency.

  • Lagos generated the highest revenue at approximately ₦7.3M and recorded the highest conversion rate at approximately 3.58%
  • Kano and Port Harcourt followed with approximately ₦6.4M each
  • Abuja and Ibadan recorded lower revenue levels at approximately ₦5.3M and ₦5.2M, with Ibadan recording the lowest conversion rate at approximately 2.83%

Conversion rates remained relatively low across all locations, indicating limited efficiency in converting customer traffic into purchases.

Product Performance by Revenue and Quantity Sold

Product performance varied significantly between revenue contribution and quantity sold.

  • Router generated the highest revenue of approximately ₦10.4M from 369 units
  • MiFi and Modem generated strong revenue of approximately ₦6.5M and ₦6.1M from lower volumes
  • SIM Pack recorded the highest quantity sold at 1,319 units but generated significantly lower revenue of approximately ₦265K

This highlights the distinction between high-value and high-volume products in driving revenue.

Product Sell-Through Rate

Sell-through rates varied across product categories.

  • SIM Pack recorded the highest sell-through rate at 53.08%
  • Router and Modem recorded moderate sell-through rates of approximately 35.23% and 32.82%
  • Broadband Device recorded the lowest sell-through rate at 10.30%

This indicates differences in product demand relative to available stock.

Stock Availability vs Units Sold

Stock levels remained significantly higher than units sold across all product categories.

  • SIM Pack: 1,327 units sold vs 2,500 units in stock
  • Broadband Device: 190 units sold despite 1,845 units in stock

This indicates excess inventory and slower product movement across categories.

7. Insights

  • Sales performance was driven by fluctuations in purchasing activity, indicating that revenue outcomes are influenced by inconsistent sales execution, with periods of stronger performance reflecting temporary improvements in demand capture rather than sustained effectiveness.
  • Revenue performance relative to target suggests that while targets were achievable, inconsistent execution led to underperformance in later periods, indicating a gap in sustaining performance rather than insufficient customer demand.
  • Store performance differences suggest that revenue outcomes are influenced by variations in sales execution across locations, resulting in uneven conversion efficiency and value extraction from customer traffic, although overall conversion remains low.
  • Product performance was driven by a mix of high value and high-volume products, indicating that revenue contribution was influenced by both pricing and demand rather than volume alone.
  • Inventory levels were higher than product demand, as excess stock and low to moderate sell through rates indicate that products are not moving fast enough to match available supply.

8. Recommendations

  • Improve sales performance by strengthening sales execution through targeted staff training focused on converting customer footfall into purchases, supported by standardized selling approaches such as needs based selling, bundle first recommendations, and structured upselling techniques to improve conversion across stores and increase revenue.
  • Increase revenue contribution by prioritizing high value products that drive revenue, while maintaining sales of high-volume products that sustain transaction activity, ensuring a balanced product focus that supports both higher transaction value and consistent sales volume.
  • Improve inventory management by aligning stock levels with customer demand, focusing on faster-moving products and reducing excess stock of slow-moving items to ensure better product movement and minimize overstock.
